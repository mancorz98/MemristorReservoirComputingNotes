\subsection{Reservoir Computing}

 
Reservoir computing (RC) is a recurrent neural network framework in which a
fixed, high-dimensional dynamical system --- the \emph{reservoir} --- projects
input signals into a rich feature space, while only a linear readout layer is
trained~\autocite{Lukoeviius2009}.
This separation of dynamics and learning dramatically reduces training cost
compared to fully trained recurrent networks, making RC attractive for
edge and neuromorphic deployments.
 
\subsubsection{General Formulation}
 
The reservoir state at discrete time step \(n\) evolves as:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{r}(n) = F\!\bigl(
    \gamma\,\mathbf{W}_{\mathrm{in}}\,\mathbf{x}(n)
    + \beta\,\mathbf{W}\,\mathbf{r}(n-1)
  \bigr),
  \label{eq:rc_state}
\end{equation}
where \(\mathbf{x}(n) \in \mathbb{R}^{d}\) is the input,
\(\mathbf{r}(n) \in \mathbb{R}^{N}\) is the reservoir state,
\(\mathbf{W}_{\mathrm{in}} \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times d}\) and
\(\mathbf{W} \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times N}\) are the fixed input and recurrent
weight matrices, \(\gamma, \beta \in \mathbb{R}^{+}\) are scaling parameters,
and \(F : \mathbb{R}^{N} \to \mathbb{R}^{N}\) is a nonlinear activation
(typically \(\tanh\)).
The trained output is a linear function of the reservoir state:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{y}(n) = \mathbf{W}_{\mathrm{out}}\,\mathbf{r}(n),
  \label{eq:rc_output}
\end{equation}
where \(\mathbf{W}_{\mathrm{out}} \in \mathbb{R}^{p \times N}\) is the sole
trainable parameter matrix, obtained by regularised least-squares regression
over a collected state matrix~\autocite{Jaeger2004}.
 
\subsubsection{Echo State Property and Spectral Radius}
 
For the readout to reconstruct any input-driven trajectory, the reservoir
must satisfy the \emph{echo state property}: its state must eventually depend
only on the driving input, not on initial conditions~\autocite{Jaeger2001}.
A necessary practical condition is that the spectral radius
\(\rho(\mathbf{W}) < 1\).
In the Echo State Network (ESN) paradigm, the recurrent matrix is
initialised with entries drawn uniformly from \([-1, 1]\) and then rescaled:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{W}^{\dagger}
  = \frac{\rho\,\mathbf{W}}{|\lambda_{\max}(\mathbf{W})|},
  \label{eq:spectral_scaling}
\end{equation}
where \(\rho \in (0,1)\) is the target spectral radius, typically chosen in
\([0.9, 1)\)~\autocite{Bai2023}.
The sparsity of \(\mathbf{W}\) is controlled by a coefficient
\(\alpha \in (0,1)\), typically \(\alpha \in [0.1, 0.3]\), which masks a
fraction of weights to zero before rescaling.
 
\subsubsection{Fading Memory and Memory Capacity}
 
The fading memory property --- that the influence of past inputs on the
current reservoir state decays over time --- is a prerequisite for stable
computation.
It is quantified by the memory capacity:
\begin{equation}
  MC = \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} MC_k
     = \sum_{k=1}^{\infty}
       \frac{\mathrm{cov}^2(u_{t-k},\,y_t)}
            {\sigma^2(u_t)\,\sigma^2(y_t)},
  \label{eq:mc}
\end{equation}
where \(MC_k\) measures the squared correlation between the output \(y_t\) and
the input delayed by \(k\) steps~\autocite{Jaeger2001}.
A large memory capacity, achievable in the asymptotically stable regime,
is essential for tasks with long temporal dependencies.
 
% -----------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Echo State Networks}
 
The Echo State Network (ESN) is the most widely studied RC instantiation for
continuous-valued time series~\autocite{Jaeger2004}.
In the leaky-integrator ESN the state update incorporates a leak rate
\(\kappa \in (0,1]\):
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{h}_t = f\!\bigl(
    (1-\kappa)\,\mathbf{W}_{\mathrm{in}}\,\mathbf{u}_t
    + \kappa\,\mathbf{W}_h\,\mathbf{h}_{t-1}
    + \mathbf{W}_{1b}\,\mathbf{y}_{t-1}
    + \mathbf{b}
  \bigr),
  \label{eq:esn}
\end{equation}
where \(\mathbf{u}_t\) is the input, \(\mathbf{y}_{t-1}\) is the optional
output feedback, \(\mathbf{W}_{1b}\) is the feedback weight matrix,
and \(\mathbf{b}\) is the bias vector~\autocite{Bai2023}.
The leak rate \(\kappa\) controls the effective time constant of the reservoir
neurons, typically set in \([0.1, 0.3]\).
 
% -----------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Next-Generation Reservoir Computing}
 
Next-Generation RC (NG-RC), proposed by Gauthier et al.~\autocite{Gauthier2021},
eliminates the recurrent network entirely by replacing the reservoir state with
an explicit feature vector constructed from time-delayed observations and their
polynomial nonlinear functionals.
The linear feature vector at step \(i\) concatenates \(k\) delay-embedded copies
of the \(d\)-dimensional input:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{O}_{\mathrm{lin},i}
  = \mathbf{X}_i \oplus \mathbf{X}_{i-s} \oplus \cdots
    \oplus \mathbf{X}_{i-(k-1)s},
  \label{eq:ngrc_linear}
\end{equation}
and a quadratic nonlinear complement is formed from the unique monomials of
the outer product:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{O}^{(2)}_{\mathrm{nonlin}}
  = \mathbf{O}_{\mathrm{lin}} \lceil\!\otimes\!\rceil\,
    \mathbf{O}_{\mathrm{lin}},
  \label{eq:ngrc_nonlinear}
\end{equation}
yielding a total feature vector
\(\mathbf{O}_{\mathrm{total}} = c\cdot\mathbf{O}_{\mathrm{lin}}
\oplus \mathbf{O}^{(2)}_{\mathrm{nonlin}}\)~\autocite{Gauthier2021}.
The output weights are trained by the same Tikhonov-regularised
least-squares as in standard RC.
The NG-RC warm-up period requires only \(sk\) time steps, and low-order
polynomial functionals are sufficient in practice due to rapid convergence
of the underlying Volterra series.

\subsection{Memristors in Reservoir Computing}
 
Memristive devices are compelling physical substrates for reservoir computing
owing to their intrinsic nonlinearity, history-dependent conductance, and
nanoscale form factor --- properties that directly fulfil the theoretical
requirements of a rich, high-dimensional reservoir with fading memory.
 
The seminal demonstration by Du et al.~\autocite{Du2017} established
memristors as viable reservoir nodes for temporal pattern recognition.
In that work, a small array of WO\textsubscript{x} memristors was driven
by amplitude-modulated pulse streams, with each memristor's conductance
serving as a reservoir state.
The system achieved 88.1\% accuracy on MNIST using 88 memristors,
demonstrating that volatile memristive dynamics can substitute for the
recurrent connectivity of a software ESN.
A key architectural feature of that and related
works~\autocite{Du2017,Hwang2025} is that the memristors are addressed
\emph{sequentially} along each row of an input image: the image is
serialised into a one-dimensional temporal stream and the reservoir is
driven by a single column of devices.
Consequently, the spatial correlations present in the two-dimensional
image are not exploited by the physical substrate; the reservoir
operates purely on the temporal sequence of pixel values.
 
The present work departs from this convention in a critical respect.
Rather than serialising the input into a one-dimensional stream,
we exploit the inherent \emph{parallel} structure of a memristor
\textbf{crossbar array}.
Each row of the crossbar is driven simultaneously by a distinct
voltage encoding a spatial patch of the input image,
and each column accumulates the nonlinear contributions from all rows
concurrently via Kirchhoff's current law.
This architecture enables the reservoir to process spatially coherent,
two-dimensional input structure in a single forward pass through the
crossbar, a modality not explored in prior memristor RC demonstrations.
The implications of this architectural choice for encoding strategy,
feature dimensionality, and classification performance are the central
subject of this paper.

\subsection{Single Device Modeling with the MMS Model}

For the simulation and design of the reservoir computing system, we use Mean Metastable Switch (MMS) memristor model introduced in~\cite{Molter2016, Minati2020}. The MMS model is a simplified representation of memristor's behavior, focusing on the average switching characteristics rather than the detailed dynamics. It captures essential features of the device by describing transitions between the high-resistance state (HRS) and the low-resistance state (LRS) based on the applied voltage. This model describes memristor's dynamics as


\begin{align}
  \label{eq:mms}
  \dert{x}       & = \frac{1}{\tau} \left( f_{\rm ON}(\ua) (1-x) - f_{\rm OFF}(\ua) x \right),              \\
  f_{\rm ON}(\ua)  & = \sigma\left(-\beta (\ua-\von)\right) \label{eq:mms5b},      \\
  f_{\rm OFF}(\ua) & = 1 - \sigma\left(\beta (\ua-\voff)\right)  \label{eq:mms5c}, 
\end{align}
where \(\sigma(z) = (1+e^{-z})^{-1}\), \(\beta= \frac{q}{kT}\), \(k\) is the Boltzmann constant, \(T\) is the absolute temperature, \(q\) represents the elementary charge, while \(\von\) and \(\voff\) denote the switching voltages to LRS and HRS states, respectively. The instantaneous memductance is defined in~\eqref{eq:conductance}.

\begin{equation}
  \Gm(x) = \frac{x}{R_{\rm ON}} + \frac{1 - x}{R_{\rm OFF}},
  \label{eq:conductance}
\end{equation}

where \(\Ron\) and \(\Roff\) denote the resistances in the low-resistance and high-resistance states, respectively.


\subsection{Crossbar Array Dynamics}

Consider an \(8 \times 8\) memristor crossbar array, where each device at position \((i,j)\) is described by the MMS model with individual parameters \(\{\Ron^{(i,j)}, \Roff^{(i,j)}, \von^{(i,j)}, \voff^{(i,j)}, \tau^{(i,j)}, \beta^{(i,j)}\}\). The state of the array is given by the matrix \(\mathbf{X} \in [0,1]^{8 \times 8}\), where \(x_{ij}\) represents the internal state of the memristor at row \(i\) and column \(j\).

\paragraph{Memductance matrix.}

The conductance of each device follows from~\eqref{eq:conductance}:
\begin{equation}
  G_{ij} = \frac{x_{ij}}{R_{\rm ON}^{(i,j)}} + \frac{1 - x_{ij}}{R_{\rm OFF}^{(i,j)}}.
  \label{eq:G_matrix}
\end{equation}


\paragraph{Column voltages.}
Let \(V_{\rm row,i}(t)\) denote the voltage applied to the \(i\)-th row, and \(R_{\rm s,j}\) the series resistance at the \(j\)-th column output. The total column conductance is
\begin{equation}
  G_{\rm col,j} = \sum_{i=1}^{8} G_{ij},
  \label{eq:G_col}
\end{equation}
and the column voltage is determined by the current-divider relation
\begin{equation}
  V_{\rm col,j} = \frac{\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^{8} V_{\rm row,i}\, G_{ij}}{G_{\rm col,j} + G_{\rm s,j}},
  \label{eq:V_col}
\end{equation}
where \(G_{\rm s,j} = 1/R_{\rm s,j}\).

\paragraph{Memristor voltage and state dynamics.}
The voltage across each memristor is
\begin{equation}
  V_{ij} = V_{\rm row,i} - V_{\rm col,j},
  \label{eq:V_mem}
\end{equation}
and its state evolves according to the MMS model~\eqref{eq:mms}:
\begin{equation}
  \dert{x_{ij}} = \frac{1}{\tau^{(i,j)}} \left( f_{\rm ON}^{(i,j)}(V_{ij})\,(1 - x_{ij}) \;-\; f_{\rm OFF}^{(i,j)}(V_{ij})\, x_{ij} \right),
  \label{eq:crossbar_dynamics}
\end{equation}
with the switching functions
\begin{align}
  f_{\rm ON}^{(i,j)}(V_{ij})  &= \sigma\!\left(-\beta^{(i,j)} (V_{ij} - V_{\rm ON}^{(i,j)})\right), \\
  f_{\rm OFF}^{(i,j)}(V_{ij}) &= 1 - \sigma\!\left(\beta^{(i,j)} (V_{ij} - V_{\rm OFF}^{(i,j)})\right).
\end{align}

The complete system consists of 64 coupled ODEs~\eqref{eq:crossbar_dynamics}, where the coupling arises through the column voltages~\eqref{eq:V_col}: each memristor's conductance \(G_{ij}\) influences \(V_{\rm col,j}\), which in turn determines the driving voltage \(V_{ij}\) for all devices in that column.

To overcome the limitations of row-wise encoding, proposed in~\autocite{Du2017} and to exploit the parallel computational structure of memristor crossbar arrays, a patch-based spatial decomposition is applied to the input images prior to temporal encoding. This approach maps localised spatial features onto the row inputs of the crossbar, enabling simultaneous reservoir processing across all rows.


Given an input image \(\mathbf{I} \in \mathbb{R}^{H \times W}\), a sliding window of size \(k_h \times k_w\) with stride \(s\) extracts a set of \(N\) patches \(\mathcal{P} = \{P_1, P_2, \ldots, P_N\}\), where each patch \(P_i \in \mathbb{R}^{k_h \times k_w}\) captures a localised spatial region. The patch at grid position \((m, n)\) is given by:

\begin{equation}
    P_{m,n} = \mathbf{I}\bigl[m s : m s + k_h - 1,\; n s : n s + k_w - 1\bigr]
\end{equation}

\noindent with \(m \in \{0, 1, \ldots, \lfloor(H - k_h)/s\rfloor\}\) and \(n \in \{0, 1, \ldots, \lfloor(W - k_w)/s\rfloor\}\). For the MNIST images (\(H = W = 28\)) with kernel size \(k_h = k_w = 8\) and stride \(s = 3\), this yields a \(7 \times 7\) grid of \(N = 49\) patches per image.



Each extracted patch \(P_i \in \mathbb{R}^{k_h \times k_w}\) is mapped onto the crossbar array such that each of the \(k_h\) rows of the patch drives a corresponding row input of the array. The \(r\)-th row of patch \(P_i\), denoted \(\mathbf{p}_i^{(r)} = [p_{r,1},\, p_{r,2},\, \ldots,\, p_{r,k_w}]\), is encoded as a temporal sequence of \(k_w\) voltage pulses with amplitudes proportional to the pixel intensities:

\begin{equation}
    V_r(t) = \sum_{c=1}^{k_w} p_{r,c} \cdot \Pi\!\left(\frac{t - (c-1)\,T_{\mathrm{period}}}{D \cdot T_{\mathrm{period}}}\right)
\end{equation}

\noindent where \(\Pi(\cdot)\) is the rectangular pulse function, \(T_{\mathrm{period}}\) is the pulse period, and \(D \in (0, 1]\) is the duty cycle. This encoding transforms each row of pixel values into an amplitude-modulated pulse train, preserving the spatial pixel ordering as a temporal sequence.

All \(k_h\) rows of a given patch are applied simultaneously, driving the corresponding row inputs of the crossbar in parallel. At each column \(c\), the column wire accumulates current contributions from all \(k_h\) row memristors according to Kirchhoff's current law, so that the terminal state of the column memristor reflects a nonlinear integration over all row signals.

\subsubsection{Feature Vector Construction}


After the complete pulse sequence for patch \(P_i\) has been applied, the terminal conductance states of all \(k_w\) column memristors are read out, yielding a local feature vector:

\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{f}_i = \bigl[x_{i,1}^{\mathrm{final}},\, x_{i,2}^{\mathrm{final}},\, \ldots,\, x_{i,k_w}^{\mathrm{final}}\bigr]^T \in \mathbb{R}^{k_w}
\end{equation}

\noindent where \(x_{i,c}^{\mathrm{final}}\) denotes the terminal state of the memristor in column \(c\) after processing patch \(i\). Each column state encodes a nonlinear transformation resulting from the simultaneous integration of all row signals at that column.

The global feature vector for a given image is formed by concatenating the local feature vectors from all \(N\) patches, with an appended bias term:

\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{x} = \bigl[\mathbf{f}_1^T,\, \mathbf{f}_2^T,\, \ldots,\, \mathbf{f}_N^T,\, 1\bigr]^T \in \mathbb{R}^{N k_w + 1}
\end{equation}

\noindent For the MNIST configuration (\(N = 49\) patches, \(k_w = 8\) columns), this yields a feature vector of dimensionality \(49 \times 8 + 1 = 393\).




\subsection{Physical Circuit Implementation}

The proposed physical reservoir computing (RC) system is centered on an \(8 \times 8\) crossbar array of memristive devices. Within this architecture, each memristor functions as a nonlinear reservoir node, where its conductance state dynamically evolves as a function of the applied voltage and intrinsic device physics.

To facilitate bidirectional communication with the crossbar array, a dedicated peripheral analog-digital interface is employed. The signal pathways and control mechanisms are defined as follows:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Input Interfacing:} Input data, such as pixel intensity values from the MNIST dataset, are mapped to proportional analog voltage signals using a DAC856 digital-to-analog converter (DAC). To ensure optimal signal integrity and adequate driving strength, these voltages are subsequently buffered by OPA427 operational amplifiers before being applied to the rows of the crossbar array, systematically stimulating the memristive nodes.
    \item \textbf{Output Readout:} The reservoir's state is evaluated by measuring the output currents from the crossbar columns. These currents are converted into measurable voltages via series-connected resistors. The resulting voltages are buffered by OPA427 operational amplifiers and digitized by an 8-channel 16-bit analog-to-digital converter (ADC) integrated within an STM32H755ZI microcontroller.
    \item \textbf{System Control and State Initialization:} The STM32H755ZI microcontroller serves as the central processing unit, orchestrating the input encoding, temporal synchronization, and readout harvesting. Furthermore, the circuit incorporates analog single-pole single-throw (SPST) switches to systematically reset the memristor conductance states between discrete input samples, ensuring independent transient responses for each inference cycle.
\end{itemize}


The idealized block diagram of the system is depicted in Figure~\ref{fig:block_diagram}, illustrating the interconnections between the PC, microcontroller, crossbar array, and peripheral components. 
The PC is responsible for preprocessing the input data (e.g., extracting pixel patches from MNIST images) and training the readout layer, while the microcontroller manages the real-time control of the DAC, ADC, and reset switches to facilitate the reservoir computing operations. The design emphasizes modularity and scalability, allowing for future expansion to larger crossbar arrays and more complex input encoding schemes. The mounted circuit is depicted in Figure~\ref{fig:photo}, showcasing the physical implementation of the described architecture.

In Figure~\ref{fig:workflow_diagram}, a detailed flowchart outlines the programming and readout procedure executed on the STM32 microcontroller for processing a single patch. The sequence begins with the reception of a patch via the serial interface, followed by \(N_{\rm p}\) programming cycles that apply reset and amplitude-modulated programming pulses to the memristors. After all programming pulses are applied, a read pulse is executed to measure the resulting conductance state, which is then transmitted back to the host PC for further processing and classification.

\subsection{Readout Layer Training}

The readout layer is a linear map
\(\mathbf{W}_{\mathrm{out}} \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times C}\),
where \(d = Nk_w + 1\) is the dimensionality of the augmented feature vector
(including an appended bias) and \(C = 10\) is the number of classes.
Only \(\mathbf{W}_{\mathrm{out}}\) is trained; the reservoir state is determined
entirely by the fixed physical dynamics of the memristive crossbar.

Prior to training, each feature dimension \(j\) is standardised
across all \(M\) training samples:
\begin{equation}
  \tilde{h}_{i,j} = \frac{h_{i,j} - \mu_j}{\sigma_j},
  \qquad
  \mu_j = \frac{1}{M}\sum_{i=1}^{M} h_{i,j},
  \quad
  \sigma_j = \sqrt{\frac{1}{M}\sum_{i=1}^{M}\!\left(h_{i,j}-\mu_j\right)^{\!2}},
  \label{eq:standardisation}
\end{equation}
yielding the normalised feature matrix
\(\tilde{\mathbf{H}} \in \mathbb{R}^{M \times (d-1)}\),
to which a column of ones is appended to form
\(\mathbf{H} \in \mathbb{R}^{M \times d}\).

\subsubsection{Ridge Regression via SVD}
\label{sec:ridge}

Let \(\mathbf{Y} \in \{0,1\}^{M \times C}\) be the one-hot encoded target matrix.
The readout weights are obtained by minimising the \(\ell_2\)-regularised
least-squares objective:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{W}_{\mathrm{out}}
  = \arg\min_{\mathbf{W}}
    \bigl\|\mathbf{H}\mathbf{W} - \mathbf{Y}\bigr\|_F^2
    + \lambda\bigl\|\mathbf{W}\bigr\|_F^2,
  \label{eq:ridge_objective}
\end{equation}
whose closed-form solution is
\(\mathbf{W}_{\mathrm{out}} = \!\left(\mathbf{H}^\top\mathbf{H}
+ \lambda\mathbf{I}\right)^{-1}\!\mathbf{H}^\top\mathbf{Y}\).

Direct inversion is numerically unstable for ill-conditioned \(\mathbf{H}\).
Instead, the solution is computed via the thin Singular Value Decomposition
\(\mathbf{H} = \mathbf{U}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\mathbf{V}^\top\),
where \(\mathbf{U} \in \mathbb{R}^{M \times r}\),
\(\boldsymbol{\Sigma} = \mathrm{diag}(\sigma_1,\ldots,\sigma_r)\),
and \(\mathbf{V} \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times r}\), with \(r = \min(M,d)\).
Substituting into the normal equations yields:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{W}_{\mathrm{out}}
  = \mathbf{V}\,\mathrm{diag}\!\left(\frac{\sigma_k}{\sigma_k^2 + \lambda}\right)
    \mathbf{U}^\top \mathbf{Y},
  \label{eq:ridge_svd}
\end{equation}
which requires no explicit matrix inversion and is numerically stable for
all \(\lambda > 0\).

The regularisation hyperparameter \(\lambda\) is selected by
\(K\)-fold cross-validation (\(K = 10\)).
To avoid recomputing the SVD for each candidate \(\lambda\),
the decomposition is computed once per fold on the training partition,
and the product \(\mathbf{U}^\top\mathbf{Y}\) is cached.
For a given \(\lambda\), evaluation reduces to two matrix multiplications
of cost \(\mathcal{O}(r^2)\), making the search over \(\lambda \in [10^{-1}, 10^{6}]\)
computationally negligible.
The optimal \(\lambda^*\) is located by scalar bounded minimisation
(Brent's method) applied to the negative mean cross-validation accuracy.

\subsubsection{Elastic Net Logistic Regression}
\label{sec:elasticnet}

As a comparative baseline, a multinomial logistic regression classifier with
Elastic Net regularisation was trained on the same feature vectors.
The optimisation problem solved for each class \(c\) is:
\begin{equation}
  \min_{\mathbf{w}_c}
  \left[
    -\frac{1}{M}\sum_{i=1}^{M} \log p(y_i = c \mid \tilde{\mathbf{h}}_i;\,\mathbf{w}_c)
    + \frac{1}{C_{\mathrm{lr}}}
      \Bigl(\alpha\|\mathbf{w}_c\|_1
           + \tfrac{1-\alpha}{2}\|\mathbf{w}_c\|_2^2\Bigr)
  \right],
  \label{eq:elasticnet}
\end{equation}
where \(C_{\mathrm{lr}} > 0\) is the inverse regularisation strength and
\(\alpha \in [0,1]\) is the \(\ell_1\) mixing ratio
(\(\alpha = 0\) reduces to ridge; \(\alpha = 1\) to lasso).
The classifier is trained with the SAGA solver~\cite{defazio2014saga},
which supports the non-smooth \(\ell_1\) term while converging at a
variance-reduced stochastic gradient rate.

The hyperparameters \((C_{\mathrm{lr}}, \alpha)\) are jointly optimised using
Optuna~\cite{akiba2019optuna} with \(10\)-fold cross-validation over
50 trials on a log-uniform grid
\(C_{\mathrm{lr}} \in [10^{-5}, 10^{2}]\) and \(\alpha \in [0, 1]\),
maximising mean cross-validation accuracy.

\subsection{Classification Performance of the Patch-Based Reservoir}
 
The patch-based reservoir was evaluated on the MNIST test set using ridge regression as the readout layer. The system achieved a classification accuracy of 87\% in the experimental verification (compared to 91\% obtained with the idealised simulation), representing a significant improvement over the 60.37\% obtained with the row-wise encoding baseline. The 4 percentage point gap between simulation and experiment can be attributed to device-to-device variability, noise in the physical readout, and non-ideal switching dynamics of the fabricated memristors, which are not captured by the idealised MMS model.
 
This performance gain can be attributed to two complementary factors. First, the patch-based decomposition preserves local two-dimensional spatial correlations that are lost under row-wise serialisation, allowing the reservoir to process spatially coherent inputs. Second, the crossbar architecture enables each column to aggregate nonlinear contributions from all rows simultaneously, producing a richer per-patch representation. The resulting 393-dimensional feature space provides the linear readout with substantially greater discriminative capacity compared to the 28-dimensional representation of the row-wise approach.
 
The confusion matrix (Fig.~\ref{fig:confusion_matrix}) shows high per-class accuracy across most digit categories, with the principal misclassifications occurring between structurally similar digit pairs such as (3,\,5) and (4,\,9). This pattern is consistent with the known morphological similarity structure of the MNIST dataset and indicates that the reservoir features capture meaningful shape distinctions.
 
The quality of the learned representations was further assessed by projecting the reservoir state vectors into two dimensions using t-SNE and UMAP (Fig.~\ref{fig:reservoir_embeddings}). Both embeddings exhibit well-separated, compact clusters corresponding to individual digit classes, confirming that the memristor reservoir maps the input patches into a feature space in which the class boundaries are approximately linearly separable.
 
These results demonstrate that memristor crossbar arrays, combined with a patch-based spatial encoding strategy, provide an effective physical substrate for reservoir computing in pattern recognition tasks. Future work will focus on optimising the patch extraction hyperparameters, exploring alternative temporal encoding schemes such as frequency modulation, and evaluating the scalability of the architecture to higher-resolution datasets and more complex classification problems.
